HAVING Clause

SQL HAVING Clause

The HAVING clause is used in SQL to filter the results of a GROUP BY operation. It is similar to the WHERE clause, but whereas WHERE filters rows before any groupings are made, HAVING filters groups after the grouping has been performed.

Basic Syntax

    SELECT columns, aggregate_function(column)
    FROM table
    GROUP BY column
    HAVING condition;
  • columns - The columns you want to retrieve from the table.
  • aggregate_function(column) - The aggregate function applied to the grouped column (e.g., COUNT, SUM).
  • table - The table from which you are retrieving data.
  • column - The column by which you want to group the data.
  • condition - The condition used to filter the grouped data.

Examples of HAVING Clause

1. Basic HAVING

Retrieve departments with more than 10 employees:

    SELECT dept_id, COUNT(*) AS num_employees
    FROM employees
    GROUP BY dept_id
    HAVING COUNT(*) > 10;

This query groups the employees by dept_id and then filters the groups to include only those with more than 10 employees.

2. HAVING with Aggregate Functions

Retrieve product categories with a total sales amount greater than $50,000:

    SELECT category, SUM(sales_amount) AS total_sales
    FROM sales
    GROUP BY category
    HAVING SUM(sales_amount) > 50000;

This query groups sales data by category and filters the results to include only categories where the total sales amount exceeds $50,000.

3. HAVING with Multiple Conditions

Retrieve employees who work in departments with more than 5 employees and where the average salary is above $60,000:

    SELECT dept_id, AVG(salary) AS avg_salary
    FROM employees
    GROUP BY dept_id
    HAVING COUNT(*) > 5 AND AVG(salary) > 60000;

This query groups employees by dept_id and applies multiple conditions to filter departments based on the number of employees and the average salary.

Advanced Usage of HAVING Clause

1. HAVING with Subqueries

Retrieve departments where the average salary is above the overall average salary of all employees:

    SELECT dept_id, AVG(salary) AS avg_salary
    FROM employees
    GROUP BY dept_id
    HAVING AVG(salary) > (
        SELECT AVG(salary)
        FROM employees
    );

This query compares the average salary of each department to the overall average salary of all employees, filtering to include only departments with a higher average salary.

2. HAVING with Sorting

Retrieve the top 5 departments with the highest total sales amount:

    SELECT dept_id, SUM(sales_amount) AS total_sales
    FROM sales
    GROUP BY dept_id
    HAVING SUM(sales_amount) > 10000
    ORDER BY total_sales DESC
    LIMIT 5;

This query groups sales data by dept_id, filters to include only those departments with a total sales amount greater than $10,000, sorts the results in descending order of total sales, and limits the results to the top 5 departments.

Summary

The HAVING clause is used to filter results after grouping data with the GROUP BY clause. It allows you to apply conditions to aggregated data, providing more refined control over the results of your queries.
