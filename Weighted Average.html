Weighted Average

A Weighted Average is a type of average where each value in the dataset contributes differently to the final result based on its assigned weight. Unlike a simple average, which gives equal importance to all values, the Weighted Average accounts for the relative importance of each value, making it a useful measure for more accurate and representative results.

Formula for Weighted Average

The formula for calculating the Weighted Average is:

Formula:
Weighted Average = (Σ (Value × Weight)) / Σ (Weight)

Where:
Value: The individual values in the dataset.
Weight: The weight assigned to each value.
Σ: The sum of the values or weights.

Example Calculation

Suppose you have the following data on student grades and their corresponding credit hours:

  • Grade in Subject 1: 80, Credit Hours: 3
  • Grade in Subject 2: 90, Credit Hours: 4
  • Grade in Subject 3: 70, Credit Hours: 2

To calculate the Weighted Average grade:

  • Multiply each grade by its respective credit hours (weight):
    (80 × 3) + (90 × 4) + (70 × 2) = 240 + 360 + 140 = 740
  • Sum of the credit hours (weights):
    3 + 4 + 2 = 9
  • Calculate the Weighted Average:
    Weighted Average = 740 / 9 ≈ 82.22

Therefore, the Weighted Average grade is approximately 82.22.

When to Use Weighted Average

  • Grades and Academic Performance: When calculating GPA or overall academic performance where different subjects or courses have different credit hours.
  • Investment Returns: When assessing the performance of a portfolio with investments of varying amounts.
  • Survey Results: When analyzing survey data where responses have different levels of importance or frequency.

Comparison with Simple Average

  • Simple Average: All values contribute equally to the final result. It's calculated by summing all values and dividing by the number of values.
  • Weighted Average: Values contribute proportionally to their assigned weights, making it more suitable for datasets with varying levels of importance.

Real-Life Applications

Weighted Average is widely used in various fields:

  • Education: Calculating weighted grades for different courses based on credit hours.
  • Finance: Determining the weighted return on investment portfolios.
  • Business: Evaluating customer satisfaction scores where different feedback categories have different impacts.
